monster: do they die at the end?

disclaimer: this is one (or four) possible reading out of many, do not take as objective fact

1. PHYSICALLY

they die at the end. let's recount the story of the storm: minato leaves during the storm to go find yori, who is badly beaten and bruised in the tub. they leave together to get back in the train car and a branch seals off the tunnel behind them. as they sit in the train car, minato's mother saori finds him missing from his bed, hearing mr hori screaming his name outside. the two reconcile and drive to the tunnel, but they are stopped by emergency services who warn them of the mudslide that's happened in the mountains. they head upwards anyway, and find that the train car has been buried in the mudslide.

meanwhile, minato and yori put their ponchos down and escape through a hole in the train car. they crawl through a tunnel, meet the light at the end of it and see that it is bright and sunny. they escape through the fields, screaming. saori and hori dig through the mud the train is buried under and find only minato's poncho and no other sign of the boys.

there is no possible way for the final scene (minato and yori screaming in the sunshine) actually happened. there are multiple contradictions that show this. when yori is in the tub, he is barely alive and we see terrible bruises on his body caused by his father beating him, and he has to be dragged out of the tub by minato on the floor, but suddenly during the scene in the tunnel he is able to walk, crawl, and climb when he could barely stand earlier. he is supposedly capable of running all the way to their hideout. yori then puts his poncho in the train car next to minato's, but when saori and hori look through the window, only minato's poncho is visible.

it is impossible to make a coherent timeline of events. at some point in time, minato and yori are in the train car during the storm, but once they emerge from it, it is bright and sunny. however, when saori and hori get to the train car, the storm is still going with no trace of them. it is not possible for them to leave and come out in the sunlight if the storm is still going when they've left.

the imagery used also points to the fact that they died at the end. when yori and minato climb up out of the tunnel, a tree falls behind them, blocking them from going backwards, signifying a "point of no return", and then they 'meet the light at the end of the tunnel' as they crawl through it. finally there's the blue gate on the train bridge that yori refers to as "the end of the line", a barrier that is completely removed by the storm when they reach it at the very final moments of the movie, as if to mark that they've crossed "the end of the line", and there's no returning to the real world.

reading it solely through this lens, they die at the end.

2. THROUGH THE ADULTS

the first two acts of this movie center around the perspectives of saori and hori. saori is minato's mother and hori is minato's teacher, and after minato begins to act unusually, saori pins the blame on mr hori's abuse of her son. an entire hour of this movie is dedicated to how these two people see the world, and how they conflict over the treatment of minato.

when saori tries to ask questions, she's met with nothing because of the bureaucratic administrative forces that stifle all productive conversation between the two leads. saori receives unhelpful and frustrating PR-safe affirmations from the principal and mr hori himself, and saori sees this as evidence that something is being hidden from her, and eventually mr hori is fired from the school.

what saori doesn't realize is that truly nothing happened and mr hori wasn't abusive, but he was unable to efficiently explain himself because the administrative forces of the school act on him as well - he is forced to rehearse his lines, he's shooed away from saori because he's not good PR, and once he gets the opportunity to explain himself, he's so frustrated that he does it tactlessly, blaming it on saori's overprotectiveness and accusing minato of having weapons at home.

hori ends up almost killing himself in the school (he has one shoe on, one shoe off as he does this), but eventually steps down from the roof and realizes what actually was happening between yori and minato during the storm. hori reconciles with saori, apologizes, explains the situation, and they try to rescue minato together.

while saori and hori are unable to speak because of the school's pressures, minato and yori are unable to be themselves because of societal pressures as a whole. there are the obvious examples of yori's dad beating him and the school bullies targeting him because he is an effeminate young boy, but the real barbs lie in subtle, one-sentence remarks that easily go missed. monster is a movie that practically functions on gossip. saori is told mr hori was at the hostess bar (he wasn't), hori is told minato was playing with the cat and assumes that minato killed it (he didn't), and hori is further told saori is an overprotective single mother (nothing in the movie shows she's overprotective, she's doing what any mother would do). and these throwaway lines serve to color each character's biases of one another, but they also serve to establish a heterosexual, boys-should-be-boys norm to yori and minato. hori tells minato "you call yourself a man?" after failing at a physical activity, hori tells the boys to "shake hands like men", saori tells minato that he should "grow up and start a family". yori is constantly bullied and called a girl or an alien (as if being an effeminate boy makes you no longer human), and the slightest bit of friendship that yori and minato share in the classroom when he passes the rag back is immediately punished by chants from the students calling them homosexuals.

minato and yori are fully cognizant of what people around them say and end up saying the same things to each other. "girls don't like boys who are afraid of the dark", "boys who know flower names are weird". this is a mindset that they have absorbed osmotically through the people around them. it prevents them from going to mr hori for support, a teacher who ultimately approved of their relationship once he realized it was even there.

now let's consider everything as a whole. hori and saori's story is the story of two people who could have very well sorted out their conflicts with clear communication, but are unable to because the administrative forces held them back and prevented them from talking through the situation with minato clearly. this results in hori almost dying, with things finally clicking into place during the storm. contrast this with the story of minato and yori, who are two young gay boys who are unable to fully be themselves with one another due to the societal structures that surround them. this leads to yori almost dying, but being rescued by minato during the storm and being taken to the train car and eventually to the fields where they are finally able to be themselves.

if hori and saori didn't die at the end and their stories parallel the boys, the boys didn't die at the end either.

3. THROUGH THE BOYS

there is almost never any positive exchange of information between a child and an adult in monster. adults and children live in separate worlds, and a child trying to tell an adult anything ends up being misconstrued. (the girl telling hori about the cat, minato telling his mother about his homosexuality but his mother confusing it for abuse). what the adults perceive as bullying, abuse, or "unusual behavior" is simply explained by the two boys' dynamic, but they cannot decode what the boys are saying, and they cannot look into the worlds the two boys live in.

the love of the two boys is necessarily hidden away from adults and encoded, sometimes figuratively, sometimes literally. minato's shoe going missing is a symbol of their relationship, but it's inscrutable to anyone looking from the outside. their repeated question of "who's the monster?", where "monster" is an evil, scary word people ascribe all sorts of negative connotations to, is just a game they play and practically how they greet each other, and yori literally writes their names backwards on essays to hide how they're together. the horn scene is the final example of this, minato screaming his love and frustration and pain out for everyone in the school to hear, but not for them to understand.

this encoding / obfuscation is further characterized by minato's avoidance and incapability to come to terms with his own homosexuality. the very first line he says is a question about the "pig's brain" hypothetical, whether or not a human is still a human if they are a homosexual - minato's mother responds "no". furthermore, the reason he is so upset after the CT scan is because he is convinced there is something wrong with him but the CT scan finds nothing. he is fully accepting of yori's homosexuality ("you were always normal"), but whenever they get too close he shies away from it, cuts his hair, even physically pushes yori away. minato does not know how to deal with his feelings for yori in any direct manner, and is only comfortable when they're only being friends and playing around. when minato is directly confronted with the rumor of yori and him being in love by his classmates, his reaction is to fight him, as if he thinks this will "undo" and hide him passing the rag to yori. minato is protective of yori and will defend him but only at a distance. instead of attacking the bullies, he throws their stuff around. instead of taking the stapler out from the door, he lets mr hori do it instead. this is him hiding his affection while still expressing it. when confronted with his real romantic feelings, he gets overwhelmed and frightened and the first thing he does is run. a subtle symbol of this is the "monster" card they use in their games. there is a single card just labelled "monster", and it is a man with so much love he has a heart for a head.

while minato takes some time to accept his homosexuality, yori has already fully accepted it. the problem is he accepts it as a disease, as something he should rightfully be punished for. the reason he wordlessly accepts the bullying from his classmates and his father is because he thinks he deserves it for having a diseased "pig's brain". as someone who's faced a lot of abuse before he's even turned 12, he views shutting down and giving up when met with abuse as a 'superpower' or a coping mechanism. it is only when minato comes to his defense, assuring him he has always been normal and does not have the brain of a pig, that he is able to reject the abuse from his father ("sorry, i lied"). this is why their relationship blossomed in the first place - minato's acceptance of yori allowed yori to be more comfortable and open around him, so much so that when minato's own confusion manifests into violence (pushing him away, fighting him in class), he still readily accepts it.

the boys talk idly about the concept of rebirth and reincarnation. they discuss the "big crunch", where everything returns back to where it was originally, and they prepare for it by decorating their hideout with planets and stars. they talk about burial, and how a proper burial will ensure smooth passage into a new life. when minato asks his mother about if his father was buried, she replies "no, he was cremated". minato's idea of reincarnation is that a person must be buried first, without being burnt or cremated, so they can have a new life. he does not take being buried to mean dying, he takes it as a release.

during the storm, when the boys get to the train car, they are buried under the mudslide. there is an especially striking scene of hori and saori digging through the mud in the window in the rain. the window is solid black, and you see small circles of rain splash onto the mud and clear it away, and then fizzle out into more mud. it looks like stars being born then being extinguished over and over again, as if it was the big crunch. the two boys get buried in the train during the big crunch and are finally reincarnated as they climb down into the canal under the train and get out to a bright, sunny day.

but what the boys say at the end seem to contradict this. "we weren't reborn, we're the same". the problem with minato's hope for reincarnation throughout the movie is that it is flawed. he wants a new world and a new life because he is frustrated and confused about his current one ("why was i born", he asks a picture of his dead father). the solution to his frustration is not reincarnation, but self-acceptance. the scene that directly precedes the storm in the final act is minato and the principal in the music room, where minato finally admits to another person that he likes yori directly with no encryption or obfuscation. this is his final act of self-acceptance, through an entire movie filled with confusion and avoidance.

through his acceptance, reincarnation no longer becomes a metaphor for radical change and a new life, but rather change inwards. as he is buried in the train car and leaves through the tunnel, he finally accepts that the problem wasn't his homosexuality but rather his perception that his homosexuality and his happiness were mutually exclusive. he says "we're the same" because all that is different now is his way of interacting with his love.

the moral of monster is ultimately one of acceptance and kindness. the movie does not shy away from the real abuse children like yori and minato face, but it graciously offers them an ability to come to terms with their own feelings. entire sections of the movie are dedicated to their cheerful young love, they are given a safe space to express it. and finally they are liberated from the oppressive forces of their school and their parents in a bright field far away from them, but they are simultaneously liberated from themselves. and they don't die at the end.

4. THROUGH FIRE AND WATER, AND THE MONSTER

monster is bookended by two extremes - the burning of a hostess bar and the torrential storm. each separate act begins with the characters looking at (or directly causing) the fire, and each act ends with all the characters caught in the storm.

with a few exceptions, almost every single article of clothing each character wears in the movie is either blue or orange, often times each outfit has both (yori has a hoodie with an orange sleeve and a blue sleeve, and minato has a blue-orange flannel). the city they live in borders a large lake and establishing shots often focus on it, and we see the characters stand near dams. hori has a fish tank with bright orange goldfish in it. the train car itself is half blue. they are meant as two halves that constitute the same whole. water and fire are considered opposing forces, but there is a duality involved with how it is portrayed - often when a character wears blue, another character wears orange.

something important about the characters in monster is that almost none of them are completely and objectively bad (except for yori's father and maybe the bullies). when we, the audience, form our impressions of each character as seen through the different perspectives, we are quick to pin down who exactly the monster is. we are first led to believe it is mr hori, then the principal, then as we learn more about their stories, a human side to them is revealed. each character is multifaceted and complex and not just monolithically evil. the best possible example of this is the principal, an old jaded woman who acts as an antagonistic force throughout the first two acts, but is revealed to be kind and accepting towards minato when they meet in the music room.

the true answer to the question "who is the monster?" is that there is no monster. even minato's drawing of the monster on his cards is on first blush a grotesque creature, but it is just a humanoid figure with a heart. no character is truly innocent or guilty, not even the boys (yori is an arsonist, minato is sometimes violent with yori). if fire is a symbol for evil and water is a symbol for good, the way each character often wears each color simultaneously represents the inherent duality and moral ambiguity of the characters.

however, to view fire as the movie's symbol for evil is reductive. yori's burning of the hostess bar is not one of pure evil, it is a crime of passion meant to free himself from his father's abuse. just look at what he does after he burns the hostess bar - he holds a toy in his hands and gleefully skips back home. when saori finds the lighter in minato's room, it's after he's torn it apart and made a mess. fire represents passion, energy, violence. it represents the burning energy saori feels when she's adamant to find out what happened to her son, and it represents the indignant anger minato feels when he throws his classmate's things around. passion can be both a force for evil as much as it is a force for good.

when the storm comes, every character is caught in it, much like every character's story intersecting at the burning of the hostess bar. if the burning of the hostess bar is what catalyzed the story, the storm is what brings it to an end. when the fire comes, each character is activated and energetic, looking at the fire from afar. when the storm comes, each character is deadened and forced to focus inwards (we see the principal staring pensively out into nowhere, and the only reason mr hori realizes the boys are together is because he has the time to think about it now). and it is here that the dynamic between the boys now switches - yori, who causes the fire, and the most active and forward of the two, is now relegated to a passivity minato has to rescue him from. (note that minato almost exclusively wears shades of blue, while yori wears shades of faded orange / pink). and when the two boys meet in the storm, minato achieves his final act of self acceptance. the movie begins with yori's passion and ends with minato's self reflection, two halves of the same whole. one couldn't exist without the other, and they make each other stronger.

the boys don't die at the end.
