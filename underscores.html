on the song geez louise!

later on in this i cover the song johnny johnny johnny. that song is about being groomed. please be mindful of that if you choose to read this all.

intro

    the wallsocket album is a deep, narratively rich concept album with much to discuss. there is an entire world here that can be explored + that i would love to explore, but i needed to write this just to get this specific part off my chest, for it is easily the most relevant to my life, and the one that had the most staggering impact on repeated listens.

  1. there are three characters, s*nny, mara, and old money bitch. this song focuses on s*nny - she is easily the most fleshed out of all three characters, and seems a clear stand-in for the author herself (april harper gray aka underscores).
  2. the song, on soundcloud, is described as a discussion between s*nny and jordan (the stand-in for also-filipina feature henhouse!). both characters are women and who live in bumfuck nowhere wallsocket michigan.
  3. i am about the same age as april (18 compared to her 23), i am filipino, and i am trans.
now that the foundation has been established, we delve into the real meat of this thing. in an effort to provide structure to my hilariously structureless thoughts i'm going to split the message of geez louise into three parts. the first part is about the erasure of philippine culture due to spanish colonialism. the second part is about transgenderism and the internet. the third part is about how these two things intersect in a sickly cruel fashion. and the secret fourth part is about how the backdrop of wallsocket michigan serves to make these issues much, much worse than they could be.

a history of filipino culture

    the philippines, before the spanish came and fucked things up, and again before the americans came and fucked it up even more, were not catholic but animist. they believed in the souls of all living and non-living things, and appointed shamans and healers to interface and communicate with these spirits (anito). the majority of these shamans were women. a minority were trans feminine, who assumed female roles in society, were treated as women, and historically married men. they were called the babaylan / asog and were not only tolerated but worshipped and respected with the same reverence a catholic respects a priest. "people like us were among the divine", s*nny laments, rightfully so - it's almost laughable to imagine society as it is now revering a trans woman.

    of course, the lyric that comes directly after "among the divine"? "until the cops arrived". the spanish conquest of the philippines all but extinguished this facet of our culture, bloodily forcing catholicism in its place - they burnt homosexuals at the stake and destroyed our shrines. today 80% of the country is catholic. we (trans people) have "existed for five hundred years", we've always been here, and we were worshipped, and now we are bathed in "murderer's blood", we are persecuted both historically and now. "people like us did it every day, until we were forced to change it."
footnote: the lyrics can be interpreted to mean the erasure of transgender culture through colonialism in general - there are many examples of this throughout the world. i just chose to focus on the philippine aspect because it's what i know about + the song is about being filipino anyway. but it sure is fucking sad we have so many examples of this eh?

    this is s*nny's take on the matter. she is mournful of the way things were, of how deeply unjust it is, of how no one ever bothered people like her until everything changed and things were irreparably damaged. her view on filipino history is colored by her personal experiences as a trans woman. jordan (henhouse!'s character) does not view it through this lens but still mourns for her native language + traditions. she's aching for her mother tongue, to adorn her skin in accidental patterns (the philippines too have a tradition of tribal tattooing that is largely gone now). from a completely cynical standpoint, none of this cultural stuff really matters - she could still live her life just fine if she assimilated wholly into american culture, abandoned all these traditions and her language, discarded all these thoughts. but jordan argues that this appreciation and exploration of your culture is "a life well lived", it's what would give her life meaning, or if not meaning, at least something to live for and love for.

    both characters love their country and love their culture and yearn for a way to express it in a lonely suburbian city almost antipodal to manila. they are lonely, fucked up diaspora who cannot interface with their culture in a way that is meaningful to them. hell, even the song is written in english.

on transgenderism in the internet age

    the way april writes s*nny's songs in wallsocket is interesting. she never really fully mentions it directly, rather, she paints a vivid background of s*nny's experiences and expects you to fill in her portrait yourself. s*nny getting laughed out of a restaurant in the video for cops and robbers, or s*nny getting groomed in johnny johnny johnny, or the vague mentions of a "benign illness" in duhhh (number of H's not accurate), while these are because she is transgender, it is not central to her experiences. april never writes about the pills, or the clothes, or the (god forbid) "playing with dolls at a young age", all these heavy-handed stereotypes that come part and parcel with transgender representation in mainstream media. the closest april gets to direct commentary is in geez louise.

    i bring this up frankly to cover my ass. this next analysis, while i feel strongly about it, i realize that i am really only basing this off of maybe three or so real lines. i recognize the possibility of me being off base here, but this is by far the most important section to me.

    in geez louise s*nny says two key phrases that stick out to me.

  • womanoid, we don't have to talk about it
  • .41 at the back of my head
"womanoid" and ".41". "womanoid" is self explanatory, ".41" less so, but i will not explain that here because i just don't want to. these are words you will hear targeted towards you if you simply spend any time as a trans person on the internet. they are meant to hurt and often do. '41' specifically is some of the worst shit you can say to someone. it was unbelievable to me to hear ".41" used in this song. it is the kind of shit you just don't throw around.

    but april uses these words not as hate. they're presented as thoughts s*nny has to deal with, just facts of her life that have stuck with her. womanoid and .41 are used to paint this background of s*nny being a trans woman who has seen experienced and seen terrible shit on the internet. someone who just simply has to deal with this abuse. they're just permanent fixtures installed in her brain now.

    i am perhaps a little overzealous in this interpretation - they're literally just 2 words. but it felt so achingly real to me as someone who has been in the same places, who grew up as a trans woman exposed to all this noxious waste. there was no handbook or guide here. i had to wade through all this shit and see all this shit and it still sticks with me and this feels like the exact same way i deal with these phrases to this day. they're just at the back of your head and you can't do anything about it.

    it is almost impossible to grow up as a trans youth and not retreat into the internet. to be trans is to live in a world actively hostile to you in a body that is even more hostile to you. people stare, people look, people hear your voice and they Know, and you hear your own voice and you Know, and it hurts. it hurts again, again, and again. it takes a long time before it stops hurting, before you come out the other side, and in this transitory state you are at your most damaged, your most vulnerable, and it is so, so easy to retreat. to go inwards and disconnect from everything outside because it hurts too much, plugging yourself into the wallsocket of the online.

footnote: the hyperpop scene. i tried to rephrase this in my own terms but this youtube comment on this video puts it far better than i ever could.

    "underscores has stated a few times in interviews and production livestreams that she is obsessed with taking something natural and giving it an artificial sheen of unreality. All kinds of vocal effects, piano and guitar licks that are impossible to physically perform, it's Baudrillard's concept of the hyperreal applied to pop music. I think this really took off with SOPHIE and her work on re-envisioning and deconstructing the body (Immaterial Girls, Faceshopping), especially the trans body, as something that is more than the sum of its parts. Then you take ideas from PC Music's hyperconsumerist satire, the concept of Pop Music as the ultimate form of consumerism (look, you can consume and purchase the concept of being popular and cool!), the fact that gender affirming surgery is an extremely expensive and invasive process, and you can kinda see this side of the hyperpop oeuvre take shape."

an aside about johnny johnny johnny

    there are two ways the internet can fuck you up as a young trans person. the first is through hate. you are taught to hate yourself in ways even cis people cannot come up with, with your 'womanoid's, your '41's, countless other things. the second is through love.

    johnny johnny johnny is a remarkably upbeat, chipper and infectious tune about being groomed. a stunning hook + instrumentals polished to perfection serve as the backdrop to a story about one of the worst things that can happen to a young child. s*nny, as a young girl, just "wants to be pretty". she wants to feel attractive and to be desired, because if maybe someone desired her body it would be beautiful enough to love it herself. she finds "creeps through the screens", and eventually lands on johnny, the much older man who will take advantage of her. johnny exploits s*nny. tells her he loves her and views her as beautiful, views her as the girl she is, solely to exploit her sexually. s*nny is desperate for affirmation here, because up til now barely anyone else has given her this affirmation. she just wants to be pretty! and he was the only one to tell her she was pretty. when he finally makes his advances she slowly and slowly realizes that something is wrong, that she shouldn't do this, and dissociates from her body so she can leave.

    johnny johnny johnny is another example of april painting this background for you. of course, this song isn't about transness - it's about being groomed. but it's clear that her being trans made her a perfect target for johnny, who took advantage of her needs and almost manipulated her into sex. you see these fragments, these little shattered mirror pieces of her life through the cuts in the glass.

    the story that april tells in johnny johnny johnny is based firmly in reality. these are real things that happened to countless of us. "discord groomer", "discord kitten" are setup and punchline. if it didn't happen to you it happened to someone you knew. april knows this, presumably has experienced it as well, and does not shy away from the reality of it all. she handles it with care, and grows a beautiful rose out of it, but does not dull its thorns. them's the breaks, man. this is what life is like for trans youth, and they deserve an artist who can capture these traumas without precluding their inherent ugliness.

there are two wolves inside me and one is a good catholic child of god and the other is an evil transsexual

    the cover of wallsocket is a horseshoe. april herself has said, whether in interviews or in the songs themselves ("it comes back around!") that horseshoe theory is a recurrent theme in the album. while the actual political concept of horseshoe theory is mostly fraud bullshit there is more than a little truth in it when separated from political contexts. two ends meeting. i bring this up because it is prominent when it comes to s*nny's faith and her transness, two foundational, rock-solid parts of her being that are inextricable yet fundamentally opposed.

    this is the part where we consider the backdrop of the album. wallsocket, michigan, bumfuck nowhere where a bank theft is enough to rock the entire community of probably about 1 thousand people. suburban hell, an extremely isolating, lonely place for someone who is both a. diaspora and b. trans to simply exist.

    when you grow up like s*nny, with barely an identity to hold onto, you cling on for dear life to what you do have - your family raised you catholic, you hang onto that. you pray the rosary before the altar and take the communion wafer on your tongue and keep the crucifix tied around your neck. you pray to god because what else is there to pray to, and maybe you even find some semblance of a community in the church. the backdrop of suburban hell acts as incentive to pursue and deepen your relationship with your faith and with god. faith is something that comes early on in childhood as well - your parents sit you down at the pews before you even have the cognitive ability to understand what's going on.

    now contrast this all with transness and wallsocket. wallsocket is actively hostile to your transness. the people there look at you funny. the people there raise confederate flags and wear maga hats and complain about "pronouns" when they think you're not listening. you are afraid to enter the correct bathrooms. you are afraid to dress how you want for fear of genuine physical harm to your person. if your parents find the pills you keep in the wardrobe they are going to kick you the fuck out. it is clear through and through that this is no space for you, that you are unsafe here. there is no one around like you and you have to figure it all out yourself (note that most trans people really only figure it out around adolescence, and some way later than that).

    these are the stones that set these magnets firmly in place, pushing against each other until one gives way. the wolves of faith and transness - which one will eat the other? the inner turmoil caused by having these two parts of your brain constantly battling each other almost needs no explanation, but april explains it anyway:

two souls in my body and mind
he doesn't make mistakes and i will not be the first one

    catholic belief dictates that god made you beautiful as you are, and desecrating yourself with transition is a sin. it is to claim that god has erred in your creation, to assume you know yourself better than god. we are often told to love ourselves, to deal with our issues in any way that isn't transition, to cure the mental illness with therapy or "accept yourself" - meaningless pieces of advice from people who do not understand and will never understand. god created man and woman, these are god-ordained titles he granted to you. who are you to shirk them?

conclusion

i have no satisfying conclusion here. i have more i want to say. i have covered two fucking songs out of twelve, and one character out of three. this is a big ass album and i have intensely flirted with the idea of getting a camera, developing this into ten thousand words, and making an honest-to-god Video Fucking Essay. i didn't even talk about the ARG.

i guess here is a good place to talk about how i actually feel about the record instead of dissecting it into numerous pieces. i like the album a lot. i like that a filipino trans woman my age is so talented and making it big in the scene and making music about the experiences we live through. i feel grateful and i hope she makes more music. her music is very good. smile :)
